Is the sense of ownership a quality of bodily sensation over and above its spatial properties?

Juan Camilo Espejo-Serna
Facultad de Filosofía y Ciencias Humanas
Universidad de la Sabana
juan.espejo1@unisabana.edu.co

NO

It seems to me that a good philosophical account of the special character of our awareness of our body "from the inside" can be found by attending to our awareness of sensation within the bounds of our felt bodies.

In particular, I think it gives us a powerful and simple account of our sense of ownership that is compatible with the empirical evidence available.

Let me explain.

Plan

  1. The Simple view of bodily ownership
  2. Referred sensations
  3. Somatoparaphrenia

The Simple view of bodily ownership

Ordinary cases of bodily awareness like that of feeling pain in my jaw involve what seems to be a sense of ownership. In feeling pain not only do I have a sensation located in a bodily part, it seems to me like it is in a part of MY body.

(This is the kind of awareness that supports IEM judgments of one's own posture. (CF Evans 1982.))

Bodily sensations may be felt as being in a body part one does not actually have (like, for example, in a phantom limb or a rubber hand) but they are felt as if they were at bodily part of one's own.

Pain in a phantom hand is perceived as a pain in what seems to be one’s hand, even if there is no such hand. Tickles in a rubber hand are perceived in what seems to one to be one's hand, even if it is a rubber hand.

(Photo from Golan Levin)

Ownership

Following Martin (1992, 1993, 1995), we might say that in so far there is bodily awareness at distinct spatial coordinates there is already a sense of one's own body. For a sensation to be felt at a particular location already is to be felt as if it were one's own body.

"What marks out a felt limb as one's own is not some special quality that it has, but simply that one feels it in this way." (Martin 1992: 201)

Simple view of bodily ownership

The body in which one feels sensations, feels as one’s own body

One may argue against the Simple view of bodily ownership by saying that there is no such awareness of ownership:

There might be awareness of one's body on the basis of which one might judge that it is our own body, but there is no proper sense of ownership

I will not discuss this form of eliminativism about the sense of ownership.

The considerations against the Simple view of bodily ownership I wish to examine are those presented by Frederique de Vignemont in her recent book Mind the Body (2018).
Two kinds of cases used by De vignemont against the Simple view of bodily ownership:
  1. Referred sensations: cases of tool-use where it is possible for a subject to non-metaphorically feel sensations in tools without having a sense of ownership of the tool as a part of their body.
  2. Somatoparaphrenia: Cases of patients suffering from a pathology may experience bodily sensations at a bodily part without experiencing that part of their body as their own.

Referred sensations

"So, further, does the woodcutter feel, along with the axe’s reaction against his hand, its hissing cleaving of the wood; so does the soldier feel his weapon piercing the flesh of his antagonist." (Lotze, 1888, pp. 588–9, quoted by De Vignemont 36)

The incorporation of tools into some body representations (Martel et al., 2016) shows that there can be some cases in which a tool might extend ones sense of one's body and yet not come with the conscious awareness of the tool as own's one like, for example, there is such awareness in the case of the rubber hand illusion.

The soldier does not consciously sense their weapon as their own body in the same conscious way in which they might feel a rubber hand in the right experimental setup.

In tool use, De Vignemont claims, a sensation can be felt at a tool and not come with the conscious awareness of the tool as one's own body. Any problem?

None, if we reflect on the ways in which our sensory consciousness might be extended.

 
It is not clear that extensions of senses work just exactly like the regular, un-extended sense. An extension can ruffle-up the distinct character of our sensation and the sense of ownership might get lost. This failure of transporting the full character happens a lot in one of the more interesting cases of extension of the senses: sensory substitution.

Subjects using a Tactile Visual Sensory Substitution (TVSS) device extend their visual capacities by exploiting the sense of touch to the extent that the device takes input through a camera and produces tactile output. (Paul Bach-y-Rita and others)

Extend?!

Yes. Their perceptual reports seem to support the idea that they can see for they recognize a range of ordinary objects, point to them in space and even tell their absolute size and distance from the subject. Some even report having a 3D sense of the environment. A good amount of what ordinary vision gives us!

And yet subjects using TVSS seem to lack a basic feature of visual experience: they sense no color, something that seems imposible in regular eyesight.

Sensory substituion suggests that even if tool use extends our capacity to have bodily sensations, there is no reason to believe that bodily sensations will have the same fundamental nature as sensations felt in our body, that is, no reason to expect that they will include a sense of ownership.

Is the sense of ownership a quality of bodily sensation over and above its spatial properties?

The evidence from referred sensations in tools does not decide in favour of De Vignemont's answer.

Somatoparaphrenia

Many [patients] can still feel pain and even cry out if the examiner pinches their “alien” hand (Melzack, 1990). For instance, one patient asked his doctor:
P: I still have the acute pain where the prosthesis is.
E: Which prosthesis?
P: Don’t you see? This thing here (indicating his left arm). The doctors have attached this tool to my body in order to help me to move [...] Once home could I ask my wife, from time to time, to remove this left arm and put it in the cupboard for a few hours in order to have some relief from pain? (Maravita, 2008, p. 102).
Some pain experiences of patients with somatoparaphrenia, also known as asomatognosia or alien hand syndrome, purportedly present a counterexample to the Simple view of bodily ownership for they seem to constitute cases of bodily sensation at one’s body without a sense of it being one’s own, that is, cases of sensation without ownership.

They seem to feel pain in another person's body!?!?!

Subjects are aware that they feel pain at a bodily part, but they are not aware of that pain as being in their own body. Some report feeling pain in a prosthetic arm; others report feeling pain in a relative’s or the examiner’s body part. It is a pain they have in a bodily part they do not sense as if it were their own.
But: it is not clear that the evidence shows bodily sensation with complete spatial character that is not felt to be in their own body.

The point: we need to tease out two aspects of spatial bodily awareness.

For even though patients suffering of somatoparaphrenia have some awareness of the spatial properties of their bodily sensations they lack an awareness of other spatial properties.

In ordinary cases, there is no question about why a bodily experience is perceived within certain bounds. In these cases, there is a sense that the limits of one’s bodily experience are those of one’s felt body, not merely to be the same as of one’s felt body but rather as constituted by it. One senses the bounds of possible experience as they are to the extent there is a sense in which the limits are set by one's own body.
So in ordinary cases, we have a spatial sense of the location of the sensation but also of the bounds within which those sensations could be felt.
But in the case of the somatoparaphrenia, subjects lack a sense of the limits of sensation: there is no sense as to why the sensation is felt at one particular place and not, for instance, three meters away.
The subjects might feel pain in a location determined by the region in space occupied by their body, but they do not sense it as so.
It is a case where the spatial structure of their bodily sensations are bound up by the sensed body but it is not presented as so, making space for confabulation about its ownership (“it is a prosthetic”, “it is my niece’s”).

So?

One might think that cases of pains in an alien hand are not genuine bodily sensations (like cases of referred sensations).

Alternatively, I think that they are better understood as cases where subjects have a limitation in their spatial sense that explains their lack of ownership.

Ordinary bodily awareness is awareness of a sensation in a region of space that is sensed as part of a bigger space determined by one’s body.

Patients with somatoparaphrenia can have awareness of a sensation in a region of space (an arm) but this space is not sensed as being part of bigger space determined by their own body.

Their sense of their body is partial, for they have no sense of the limits of possible sensation.

Is the sense of ownership a quality of bodily sensation over and above its spatial properties?

The evidence from somatoparaphrenia does not decide in favour of De Vignemont's answer.

To conclude: The special character of bodily awareness as having a sole object, in contrast to the five traditional senses, is perhaps over-emphasized.

Though bodily awareness does not involve identifying one among many possible objects of experience, more than just the body is presented to one.

Our bodily awareness is like simultanagnosia.One directly senses only the body, but our bodily awareness also grants us a sense of limits that is more than just what affords a sensed object.
