The sense of ownership in bodily awareness as a sense of space

Juan Camilo Espejo-Serna
Facultad de Filosofía y Ciencias Humanas
Universidad de la Sabana
juan.espejo1@unisabana.edu.co

It seems to me that a good philosophical account of the special character of our awareness of our body "from the inside" can be found by attending to our awareness of sensation within the bounds of our felt bodies.

In particular, I think it gives us a powerful and simple account of our sense of ownership that is compatible with the empirical evidence available.

Let me explain.

The Simple view of bodily ownership

Ordinary cases of bodily awareness like that of feeling pain in my jaw involve what seems to be a sense of ownership. When feeling pain not only do I feel pain located in a bodily part, it seems to me like it is in a part of MY body.
For some philosophers the sense of bodily ownership is intrinsic to an awareness of the body “from the inside”, so that where there is such bodily awareness there is also an awareness as of MY body.
Bodily sensations may be felt as being in a body part one does not actually have (like, for example, in a phantom limb or a rubber hand) but, the claim is, they are felt as if they were at bodily part of one's own.

Even pain in a phantom limb is perceived as a pain in what seems to be one’s limb.

(Photo from Golan Levin)

Simple view of bodily ownership

The body in which one feels sensations, feels as one’s own body

The idea here is that in so far there is bodily awareness at distinct spatial coordinates there is already a sense of one's own body. For a sensation to be felt at a particular location already is to be felt as if it were one's own body.
One may argue against the Simple view of bodily ownership by saying that there is no such awareness of ownership:

There might be awareness of one's body on the basis of which one might judge that it is our own body, but there is no proper sense of ownership

I will not discuss this form of eliminativism about the sense of ownership.

The considerations against the Simple view of bodily ownership I wish to examine are those presented by Frederique de Vignemont in her recent book Mind the Body (2018).

1. Referred sensations: cases of tool-use where it is possible for a subject to non-metaphorically feel sensations in tools without having a sense of ownership of the tool as a part of their body

2. Somatoparaphrenia: Cases of patients suffering from pathology may experience bodily sensations in their body without experiencing their body as their own.

Referred sensations

Consider the case of a blind person feeling the roughness of the ground and the tip of their cane.

This, De Vignemont claims, is a case where a sensation is felt but it is not as if it were in a part of one's own body.

These cases, De Vignemont argues, should not be understood as instances of indirect perception as those of being aware the mailperson is here by hearing the dog barking.

Rather, they should be understood as cases of direct awareness, like that of being aware of the mailperson has arrived by hearing their voice.

One may think that the cases of referred sensations in tools are like what O’Shaughnessy calls a transportation of appearance (2008, p. 395) where, thanks to the tool, there is a reliable transportation of what would be sensed were one in a different spatial location.
Just like a telescope transports the appearance of faraway objects for us to see as if they were here, we can think of canes as transporting what would be a bodily sensation we would have if our body extended as far as the tool goes.
And yet: if referred sensations are understood as an extension by transportation of our sensory capacities it is not clear that De Vignemont’s conclusion follows.
 
It is not clear that extensions of senses work just exactly like the regular, un-extended sense. An extension might ruffle-up the distinct character of our sensation and the sense of ownership might get lost.

Subjects using a Tactile Visual Sensory Substitution (TVSS) device have extended their visual capacities by exploiting the sense of touch. And some of their perceptual reports seem to support the idea that they can recognize a range of ordinary objects, point to them in space and even tell their absolute size and distance from the subject. Some even report having a 3D sense of the environment. A good amount of what ordinary vision gives us!

And yet subjects using TVSS seem to lack a basic feature of visual experience: they sense no color.

So: even if tool use extends our capacity to have bodily sensations in an object different from our own body, the evidence does not show that those bodily sensations will have the same fundamental nature as sensations felt in our body, that is, no reason to expect that they will include a sense of ownership.

Sensations felt in tools might be more like a visual awareness of our own body (e.g., seeing that our body is hurting) than like the bodily awareness of it.

Referred sensations might not be bodily sensations proper and thus might not pose a genuine counter example.

Somatoparaphrenia

Some pain experiences of patients with somatoparaphrenia, also known as asomatognosia or alien hand syndrome, purportedly present a counterexample to the Simple view of bodily ownership for they seem to constitute cases of bodily sensation at one’s body without a sense of it being one’s own body, that is, cases of sensation without ownership.
Many [patients] can still feel pain and even cry out if the examiner pinches their “alien” hand (Melzack, 1990). For instance, one patient asked his doctor:
P: I still have the acute pain where the prosthesis is.
E: Which prosthesis?
P: Don’t you see? This thing here (indicating his left arm). The doctors have attached this tool to my body in order to help me to move [...] Once home could I ask my wife, from time to time, to remove this left arm and put it in the cupboard for a few hours in order to have some relief from pain? (Maravita, 2008, p. 102).
Subjects are aware that they feel pain at a bodily part, but they are not aware of that pain in their own body. Some report having pain in a prosthetic arm; others report feel pain in a relative’s or the examiner’s body part.
I do not think this philosophical gloss on the reports is obviously correct. It is not clear that the evidence shows bodily sensation in a body but not in an owned body. In the following I present a way it might rather be.
Even though patients suffering of somatoparaphrenia have some awareness of the spatial properties of their bodily sensations they lack an awareness of other spatial properties> those of the limits.
In ordinary cases, there is no question about why a bodily experience is perceived within certain bounds. In these cases, there is a sense that the limits of one’s bodily experience are those of one’s felt body, not merely as coinciding with one’s body but rather as constituted by it. One senses the bounds of possible experience as they are to the extente there is a sense in which the limits are set by one's own body.
In the case of the somatoparaphrenia, subjects lack a sense of the limits of sensation: there is no sense as to why the sensation is felt at one particular place and not, for instance, three meteres away.
The subjects might feel pain in a location determined by the region in space occupied by their body, but they do not sense it as so. The spatial structure of their bodily sensations might be tied up to their body but is not presented as so and thus lends itself to confabulation about its ownership (“it is a prosthetic” “it is my niece’s”).

Ordinary bodily awareness is awareness of a sensation in a region of space that is sensed as part of a bigger space determined by one’s body.

Patients with somatoparaphrenia can have awareness of a sensation in a region of space that is part of a bigger space, but this space is not sensed as being part of bigger space determined by their own body. Their sense of their body is partial, for they have no sense of the limits of possible sensation.

So> it might be possible to think that cases of pains in alien hands are not genuine bodily sensations. It is unclear. Rather, I think that they are better understood as cases where there is a limitation in their spatial sense that explains their lack of ownership.
De Vignemont’s purported counterexamples aim to show that there is more to the sense of ownership of one's body than sensations and their spatial properties. But I have shown that a reasonable alternative is that the cases demonstrate the rather the disassociation of ownership and some bodily spatial properties. I have argued against the plausibility of her reading of these cases by showing an alternative that leaves open the possibility of identifying the sense of ownership with a distinct form of spatial awareness.
